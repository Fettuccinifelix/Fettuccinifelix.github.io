Breast Cancer QA Project

← Back to Home

Background and Overview

This project, completed during my research term at BC Cancer, focuses on developing a quality assurance (QA) tool for evaluating internal mammary lymph node (IMN) contours generated by Limbus Contour, a deep learning-based auto-segmentation software. The tool supports a retrospective cohort of approximately 19,000 breast cancer patients (2005–2014) and aims to improve dosimetric studies linking IMN dose, tumor location, and survival outcomes.

1.0 Project Objectives

  • Develop a QA pipeline to assess fidelity of AI-generated IMN contours.
  • Establish a reliable ground-truth set for evaluation.
  • Support retrospective research into the effects of IMN irradiation on survival outcomes.

2.0 Methodology

2.1 Implementation

In-house scripts were written in C# using the Eclipse Scripting API (ESAPI) to analyze contour quality. The QA tool flagged patients with contours outside of statistically defined ranges (median ± 2SD, Min/Max, IQR) or with structural faults such as missing slices.

2.2 Evaluation Approaches

  • Method A: Compared AI contours against clinical contours. This approach was unreliable due to inconsistent contouring guidelines (RTOG vs ESTRO) and incomplete slices.
  • Method B: Compared AI contours to a reviewed subset of 100 Limbus-generated contours (RTOG guideline), providing a consistent baseline.

2.3 Technical Challenges

During contour transfer, ESAPI’s Structure.SegmentVolume() occasionally shifted contours in cases where CT dimensions varied due to couch insertions. This was resolved by directly copying coordinates slice-by-slice. Ratios such as IMN:Lung and IMN:Chest Wall were introduced to normalize contour dimensions across patients of varying sizes.

3.0 Results

  • Method A flagged 3/100 contours.
  • Method B flagged 32/100 contours.
  • Clinician review flagged 47/100, with Z-dimension errors (intercostal space length) as the most common fault.
  • The QA tool misidentified ~25% of cases, depending on thresholds and metrics.

4.0 Findings and Discussion

Clinical contours were too inconsistent to serve as a reliable ground-truth. The reviewed subset of Limbus contours was more effective for QA. The most common failure mode was incorrect Z-dimension contouring. Absolute length metrics introduced patient-size bias, making normalized ratios a better approach.

5.0 Next Steps

  • Expand the dataset used for defining clinically acceptable ranges.
  • Incorporate anatomical context (e.g., relative placement to chest wall or lungs).
  • Integrate QA directly into the pipeline for real-time flagging of errors.
  • Validate the tool across larger and independent patient cohorts.
